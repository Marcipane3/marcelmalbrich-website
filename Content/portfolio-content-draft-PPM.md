## 1. PPM Rollout — Project Portfolio Management System

**What it was**
A multi-year implementation of a Power Apps-based Project Portfolio Management (PPM) system across a global organization's project management function, spanning five international sites. The system replaced fragmented, site-specific tracking with a unified platform covering the full lifecycle from contract setup through invoicing.

**What I did**
- Acted as the bridge between business stakeholders (PMs, finance, BD) and the implementation/IT side — translating operational needs into system requirements and translating system capabilities back into process changes people could actually adopt
- Led process modeling and redesign for core financial workflows (contract-to-invoice) and operational workflows (resource/slot scheduling), establishing naming conventions, data governance standards, and SOPs adopted across sites
- Designed and oversaw Power BI reporting (KPI dashboards, data quality monitoring, project health overviews) — managing two student developers who built the reports against specs I defined
- Built and documented Power Automate flows for cross-functional notifications (e.g., automated invoice triggers between project management and finance teams)
- Drove site-by-site rollout: training, documentation, and super-user enablement across sites in Europe, the US, and Asia — adapting communication style for cultural and operational differences at each site
- Authored a multi-year platform roadmap structuring future development into prioritized phases, balancing technical feasibility against organizational change capacity

**What I learned**
- The hardest part of a system rollout isn't the configuration — it's getting five sites with different working cultures to agree on one way of doing things. Standardization only works if the standard makes someone's job easier on day one, not just "eventually."
- Good documentation is a force multiplier: every question a user has to ask twice is a documentation gap, not a training gap.
- Translating between "what the business needs" and "what the system can do" is a skill in itself — and the most valuable role on a project like this is often the person who can do both, not the person who can do either one best.
- A roadmap full of features isn't a proposal. Decision-makers need named, scoped bundles with clear trade-offs — not a wish list.

**Note on confidentiality:** keep this version generic (no company name, site names abstracted to "five international sites," no specific tooling vendor named beyond "Power Platform" if needed). Confirm scope with current employer before publishing. If cleared, "pharmaceutical CDMO" and "five global sites" would add credibility without revealing much.

## 2. Festival Organization — Bordenau Village Festival

**What it was**
Organized a ~1,000-guest cultural festival in my hometown of Bordenau, as part of a broader village development and community engagement effort.

**What I did**
- End-to-end event planning: logistics, vendor coordination, scheduling, on-the-day operations
- Coordinated volunteers and local stakeholders (council, businesses, residents) to deliver an event at a scale well beyond typical village capacity
- Balanced cultural programming goals with practical constraints — budget, permits, safety, weather contingencies
- Managed the project as a one-shot deliverable with a hard deadline and no room for slippage — high-stakes, single-attempt execution

**What I learned**
- Running a ~1,000-person event with a volunteer team taught me more about stakeholder management under pressure than most formal project work — everyone has a day job, nobody reports to you, and the deadline doesn't move
- Contingency planning matters more than the plan itself — the value is in having already thought through what happens when something goes wrong
- Community projects succeed or fail based on whether people feel ownership, not whether the plan is good on paper

## 3. Cookbook App — AI-Assisted Side Project

**What it was**
A personal side project: a cookbook/recipe app built largely through AI-assisted ("vibe coded") development — using AI tools to accelerate building a working application from an idea to a functional product.

**What I did**
- Defined the product concept and feature scope
- Worked iteratively with AI coding assistance to build, debug, and refine the application
- Made architecture and tooling decisions even while delegating implementation details — staying in control of the "what" and "why" while leveraging AI for the "how"

**What I learned**
- AI-assisted development changes the skill that matters most: it's no longer about writing every line, it's about knowing what to ask for, recognizing when the output is wrong, and being able to debug something you didn't write yourself
- Building something end-to-end — even a small project — surfaces a hundred small decisions that never come up when you're just reviewing other people's specs
- This is a useful proof point for the "technically literate, not a developer" positioning: I can ship working software without that being my day job

## 4. henmarsell — Online Shop (Co-Founded)

**What it was**
Co-founded an e-commerce business selling gardening and aquarium products (notably the "Fischturm/Fischsäule" — a fish column/tower product), sold via Amazon and a dedicated standalone site.

**What I did**
- Co-founded and helped build the business from concept to live sales channels
- Set up and managed listings across Amazon and a dedicated .eu storefront
- Handled the practical realities of running a small e-commerce operation: product positioning, channel management, operational logistics

**What I learned**
- Entrepreneurship is mostly logistics and iteration, not big strategic moves — small consistent decisions about listings, pricing, and fulfillment compound
- Running something end-to-end, even at small scale, builds a different kind of confidence than working within someone else's system — you own every outcome, good or bad
- This is the clearest "I've built and run something real, with my own money and time at risk" story in my portfolio — useful counterweight to the corporate PM narrative
